\begin{abstract}
Spatial transcriptomics reveals the positions of thousands of cell types across
tissue, but current analysis tools either operate at a single spatial scale or
lack interpretable statistical summaries. Here we introduce a multiscale spatial
fingerprinting framework that quantifies how cell types are organized across
spatial scales from \SI{40}{\micro\meter} to \SI{1000}{\micro\meter} in the
mouse brain. Using Allen Brain Cell Atlas MERFISH data
(\num{1374804} cells across isocortex, hippocampus, and thalamus), we compute
variance-to-mean ratio (VMR) fingerprints for each cell type at 12 logarithmically
spaced scales, tested against label-shuffle null models via covariance-aware
Hotelling's $T^2$ statistics.

We show four principal results.
First, cell types exhibit robust, grid-placement-independent spatial fingerprints
whose effect sizes span two orders of magnitude---from sub-Poisson regularity
(peak VMR ratio $0.95\times$ null) to extreme clustering
($36.4\times$ null)---across three brain regions.
Second, subclass-level fingerprints in isocortex recover known laminar
architecture, with excitatory subtypes showing layer-specific characteristic
clustering scales, while thalamic subtypes show distinct nuclear
compartmentalization signatures.
Third, cross-type co-occurrence is scale-dependent: among 28 curated
subclass pairs, isocortical layer types predominantly co-localize
(96--100\% of pairs show positive correlation, depending on scale),
whereas thalamic nuclear types show mixed, weak associations
(46--64\% positive), consistent with the distinction between layered
and nuclear brain architectures.
Fourth, stratum-level mediation analysis decomposes the origin of these
fingerprints: cortical layer assignment mediates a median of 78.1\% of
native isocortical VMR signal (64.5\% including mislocated types from
adjacent regions), while thalamic nuclear membership mediates 91.0\%.
Neuronal types show near-complete mediation (excitatory: 90--94\%; thalamic:
97--100\%), whereas glial and vascular types retain substantial within-stratum
organization (vascular mediation: 39\% isocortex, $-44$\% thalamus),
revealing that the organizing principles differ qualitatively between cell
classes and brain architectures.

The same VMR/chi-squared framework previously applied to protein nanoclusters
(SMLM, nm scale) and synaptic clustering on dendrites (\si{\micro\meter} scale)
here detects meaningful structure at the tissue scale (\si{\milli\meter}),
supporting a universal spatial fingerprinting approach across biological scales
and geometries. The mediation analysis extends a framework previously used to
decompose dendritic synaptic organization into partner-level mechanisms to the
tissue scale, demonstrating that stratum-level structure explains the majority
of neuronal fingerprints across both layered and nuclear brain architectures.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

% ── The problem ──
Spatial transcriptomics technologies---including MERFISH, seqFISH+, Slide-seq,
and Visium---now routinely resolve the positions and transcriptomic identities of
millions of cells across tissue sections \citep{Zhang2023, Fang2022}. A central question
emerging from these datasets is: \emph{how are cell types spatially organized,
and at what scales does that organization manifest?}

% ── Existing tools and their limitations ──
Current spatial analysis tools address parts of this question. Squidpy
\citep{Palla2022} computes neighborhood enrichment at a fixed spatial
neighborhood radius. SpatialDE \citep{Svensson2018} identifies spatially variable
genes but operates gene-by-gene, not type-by-type. BANKSY \citep{Singhal2024}
incorporates spatial context into clustering but does not provide
scale-resolved statistics. More recently, SPACE \citep{Schrom2025} uses information-theoretic methods to
detect complex multi-type spatial patterns, and CellCharter \citep{Varrone2024}
identifies spatial cell niches through graph-based clustering. However, none of
these tools produce continuous \emph{multiscale spatial fingerprints}---curves
that summarize how a cell type's clustering varies continuously from fine
($\sim$\SI{40}{\micro\meter}) to coarse ($\sim$\SI{1000}{\micro\meter})
resolution, nor do they provide a mediation decomposition identifying which
organizing variables generate the observed spatial structure.

% ── Our approach ──
Here we apply a multiscale variance-to-mean ratio (VMR) framework, previously
developed for protein nanocluster detection in super-resolution microscopy and
synapse clustering on dendritic trees, to the tissue scale. The key idea is
simple: at each spatial scale, tile the tissue into non-overlapping grid cells,
count instances of a cell type per cell, and compute the VMR of those counts.
For a completely random (Poisson) distribution, VMR~$= 1$; clustering produces
VMR~$> 1$. By sweeping across scales, we obtain a \emph{spatial fingerprint}
that encodes where and how strongly a cell type clusters.

% ── Null model ──
Significance is assessed via label-shuffle null models---the strongest available
control, which preserves all spatial structure and only permutes type-to-position
assignments. The full VMR curve is tested jointly using a covariance-aware
Hotelling's $T^2$ statistic with Ledoit--Wolf shrinkage regularization.

% ── Scope ──
Beyond detection, we ask: \emph{what generates these fingerprints?} If cell types
are organized primarily by stratum membership (cortical layer or thalamic nucleus),
then shuffling type labels only within each stratum should abolish the fingerprint.
We formalize this as a mediation analysis: the proportion of the overall VMR signal
that is absorbed by within-stratum shuffling measures how much of a type's spatial
organization is explained by its stratum distribution versus finer-scale patterning
within strata. This approach extends the mediation framework previously used to
decompose synaptic spatial organization on dendrites into partner-level mechanisms
\citep{voruganti2026coupling}.

We apply this framework to three brain regions from the Allen Brain Cell Atlas
MERFISH dataset: isocortex (\num{936281} cells), hippocampal formation
(\num{304736} cells), and thalamus (\num{133787} cells). We analyze per-type
fingerprints, cross-type co-occurrence, regional comparisons, stratum-level
mediation, and a focused comparison of layered (isocortex) versus nuclear
(thalamus) organizational motifs.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

We used the Allen Brain Cell Atlas MERFISH dataset (MERFISH-C57BL6J-638850)
\citep{Zhang2023}, which provides spatial coordinates and transcriptomic cell
type annotations for \num{3938808} cells across the adult mouse brain. Cells
are annotated at three hierarchical levels: 34 classes, 338 subclasses, and
1,201 supertypes.

Brain region assignments were obtained by merging cell coordinates with the
Allen Common Coordinate Framework (CCF v3) parcellation. We analyzed three
regions: isocortex (CCF division ``Isocortex''), hippocampal formation (``HPF''),
and thalamus (``TH''). Tissue maps are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:tissue_maps}.

\subsection{Multiscale VMR Fingerprints}
\label{sec:vmr}

For a given cell type $t$ with positions $\{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{n_t}$:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Construct a 2D density grid ($256 \times 256$ pixels) over the region
    of interest via histogram binning.
  \item For each spatial scale $s$ in a set of 12 logarithmically spaced
    scales from \SI{40}{\micro\meter} to \SI{1000}{\micro\meter}:
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item Partition the grid into non-overlapping cells of side length $s$.
      \item Count the number of type-$t$ cells per grid cell.
      \item Compute $\vmr(s) = \mathrm{Var}(c) / \mathrm{Mean}(c)$.
    \end{enumerate}
  \item The resulting curve $\vmr(s)$ is the \emph{spatial fingerprint} of type~$t$.
\end{enumerate}

Scale selection uses the \texttt{ScaleRange.for\_tissue()} algorithm, which
generates candidate scales in log-space, converts to grid voxel sizes, deduplicates
to ensure each scale produces a distinct binning resolution, and enforces a
minimum resolvable scale of $2 \times (\text{ROI size} / \text{grid size})$.

\subsection{Null Models and Statistical Testing}
\label{sec:null}

\paragraph{Label-shuffle null.}
For each of 50 realizations, cell type labels are randomly permuted while
positions remain fixed. This preserves all spatial structure (density gradients,
tissue geometry) and tests specifically whether type-to-position assignments are
non-random.

\paragraph{Covariance-aware chi-squared test.}
The observed VMR curve $\mathbf{v}_\text{real}$ is compared to the null
distribution $\{\mathbf{v}_k\}_{k=1}^{K}$ using Hotelling's $T^2$:
\begin{equation}
  T^2 = \frac{K}{K+1} (\mathbf{v}_\text{real} - \bar{\mathbf{v}})^\top
  \hat{\Sigma}^{-1}_\text{reg}
  (\mathbf{v}_\text{real} - \bar{\mathbf{v}})
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\Sigma}_\text{reg} = (1-\lambda)\hat{\Sigma} + \lambda \,
\mathrm{diag}(\hat{\Sigma})$ is the Ledoit--Wolf shrinkage-regularized
covariance ($\lambda = 0.02$). The $T^2$ statistic is converted to an
$F$-statistic with $(p, K-p)$ degrees of freedom.

\paragraph{Effect-size metrics.}
To avoid over-reliance on $p$-values in this high-powered setting, we report:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Peak VMR ratio}: $\max_s \vmr_\text{real}(s) / \vmr_\text{null}(s)$
  \item \textbf{Integrated excess}: $\int [\vmr_\text{ratio}(s) - 1] \, d(\log s)$
  \item \textbf{Fingerprint width}: scale range (in decades) over which VMR ratio
    exceeds half its peak
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cross-Type Co-occurrence}
\label{sec:cooccurrence}

For each pair of cell types $(A, B)$ at each scale $s$, we compute the Pearson
correlation of their binned counts across grid cells:
\begin{equation}
  r_{AB}(s) = \mathrm{Corr}\bigl(c_A^{(s)}, c_B^{(s)}\bigr)
\end{equation}
Positive values indicate co-localization; negative values indicate segregation.
Significance is assessed by comparing against the label-shuffle null distribution.
With 28 curated pairs $\times$ 12 scales $= 336$ tests, we note that individual
$p$-values should be interpreted cautiously; we focus on effect-size patterns and
fraction-positive summaries rather than individual significance calls.

\subsection{Grid Jitter Robustness}
\label{sec:jitter}

To verify that results are not artifacts of arbitrary grid placement, we repeated
the full analysis 10 times with random grid origin offsets drawn from
$\mathrm{Uniform}(-2\Delta, +2\Delta)$ in each axis, where $\Delta$ is the
grid voxel size. We report the coefficient of variation (CV) of peak VMR ratio
across jitter trials.

\subsection{Stratum-Level Mediation Analysis}
\label{sec:mediation}

To decompose each cell type's spatial fingerprint into stratum-explained and
within-stratum components, we applied a within-stratum label-shuffle null model.
For isocortex, the stratum variable is cortical layer (6 layers: L1, L2/3, L4,
L5, L6a, L6b); for thalamus, it is thalamic nucleus (46 nuclei from the CCF
parcellation).

Within each stratum, cell type labels were randomly permuted while spatial
positions remained fixed. This preserves all within-stratum density structure and
tests whether type-to-position assignments carry information beyond stratum
membership. We computed VMR fingerprints under 50 within-stratum shuffle
realizations and tested the residual signal using the same Hotelling's $T^2$
framework.

Mediation percentage was computed as:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Mediation}_\% = 100 \times \left(1 - \frac{\chi^2_\text{within-stratum}}{\chi^2_\text{overall}}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\chi^2_\text{overall}$ is the Hotelling's $T^2$ statistic from the
standard (unconstrained) label-shuffle null and $\chi^2_\text{within-stratum}$
is from the within-stratum shuffle. High mediation indicates that stratum
membership explains most of the spatial fingerprint; low or negative mediation
indicates substantial within-stratum organization not captured by stratum
assignment.

\subsection{3D Thalamic Fingerprints}
\label{sec:3d}

To assess the impact of projecting 3D brain structure to 2D, we computed 3D VMR
fingerprints for thalamus using \texttt{np.histogramdd} with a $64 \times 64
\times 64$ grid (chosen to approximate the 2D resolution of the $256 \times 256$
grid in volumetric terms). The same label-shuffle null and Hotelling's $T^2$
framework were applied. We report the ratio of 3D to 2D peak VMR ratios for each
cell type as a measure of z-axis information content.

\subsection{Software and Reproducibility}

All analyses were implemented in Python using NumPy, SciPy, Pandas, and
Matplotlib. Data access used the \texttt{abc\_atlas\_access} package. Code,
results, and figures are available at
\url{https://github.com/saivoru777-ship-it/spatial-transcriptomics-fingerprints}.

% ── Result 4 ──
\subsection{Stratum-level mediation decomposes spatial fingerprints}
\label{sec:result4}

To determine how much of each cell type's spatial fingerprint is explained by
stratum membership, we applied within-stratum label-shuffle mediation analysis
to isocortex (6 cortical layers) and thalamus (46 nuclei)
(Table~\ref{tab:mediation}).

\textbf{Isocortex.} Cortical layer assignment mediates a median of 64.5\% of
the VMR signal across 15 cell types (mean 54.4\%). Neuronal types show the
strongest mediation: IT-ET Glut (90.2\%), NP-CT-L6b Glut (94.4\%), and CTX-MGE
GABA (PV/SST-like, 90.2\%) are almost entirely explained by layer.
CTX-CGE GABA (VIP/Lamp5-like) retains more within-layer organization (77.6\%
mediation). Glial types show substantial but incomplete mediation
(Astro-Epen 78.6\%, OPC-Oligo 64.5\%, Immune 70.9\%). Vascular cells show the
lowest mediation (38.6\%), consistent with a vascular network that is distributed
across layers rather than layer-confined. One type shows negative mediation
(CNU-HYa Glut, $-23.1$\%): this is a mislocated hypothalamic type whose
within-layer distribution is \textit{more} structured than its overall
distribution, because within-layer shuffling disrupts its confinement to specific
cortical zones.

\textbf{Thalamus.} Nuclear membership mediates a median of 91.0\% across 12 cell
types (mean 79.9\%). Native thalamic neurons show near-complete mediation: TH Glut
(99.1\%), MH-LH Glut (100.0\%). Non-native types from adjacent regions also show
high mediation (HY GABA 99.5\%, MB GABA 99.0\%, MB Glut 97.8\%), confirming that
their thalamic presence reflects nuclear-boundary confinement. Glial types retain
more within-nucleus organization (Astro-Epen 73.1\%, OPC-Oligo 54.4\%). Vascular
cells again show negative mediation ($-43.5$\%), indicating spatially homogeneous
distribution that is \textit{disrupted} by within-nucleus shuffling.

\textbf{Cross-architecture comparison.} The isocortex median of 64.5\% includes
mislocated types from adjacent regions (e.g., OB-IMN GABA, 10.3\%; CNU-HYa Glut,
$-23.1$\%) whose low mediation reflects their spatial confinement to non-cortical
zones. Restricting to region-native types (the 8 types with primary isocortical
identity), the median rises to 78.1\%. The thalamus shows systematically higher
mediation than isocortex (median 91.0\% vs.\ 78.1\% native-only). This is consistent with
the distinct organizational motifs of the two regions: thalamic nuclei are
discrete, non-overlapping territories that almost fully determine cell type
identity, whereas cortical layers share cell types across columns and have
more graded transitions. The mediation analysis quantifies this architectural
difference: nuclear membership is a stronger organizing variable than layer
membership.

\textbf{Continuous depth within layer.} A potential concern is that the 35\%
isocortical residual reflects imperfect discretization of a continuous
depth gradient into 6 discrete layers. To test this, we created compound strata
by binning the z-coordinate (cortical depth) into quintiles within each layer
(30 strata total) and repeated the mediation analysis. Adding continuous depth
increases mediation by a median of $+3.8$ percentage points for the major
neuronal and glial types: IT-ET Glut $+4.0$\%, NP-CT-L6b Glut $+1.5$\%,
Astro-Epen $+5.4$\%, CTX-MGE GABA $-3.4$\%, CTX-CGE GABA $-0.2$\%.
One outlier (OPC-Oligo: $-67.8$\%) likely reflects chi-squared instability
from over-partitioning into sparse compound strata; excluding this, the
range is $-10.2$ to $+17.2$ percentage points. This confirms that discrete
layer labels capture most of the depth-organized signal, and the isocortical
residual primarily reflects non-depth spatial structure (tangential
organization, local niche features) rather than a discretization artifact.

\textbf{3D versus 2D fingerprints.} To assess whether the 2D projection loses
z-axis information, we computed 3D VMR fingerprints for thalamus. The mean
3D/2D peak VMR ratio was 1.32, indicating that 3D fingerprints are on average
32\% stronger. The effect is cell-type-specific: midbrain types show the largest
gains (MB Glut: $1.94\times$, MB GABA: $1.90\times$, HY GABA: $1.90\times$),
consistent with strong z-axis lamination of types entering the thalamus from
ventral/dorsal boundaries. Native thalamic types show a slight \textit{decrease}
(TH Glut: $0.87\times$, MH-LH Glut: $0.77\times$), likely because their
broad nuclear distribution is better captured by a 2D projection that integrates
across z. Glial and vascular types are essentially unchanged
(OPC-Oligo: $1.00\times$, Vascular: $0.99\times$). The main findings
(detection of spatial fingerprints, layered vs.\ nuclear distinction, mediation
results) are therefore robust to the 2D projection, though z-laminated types
benefit from 3D analysis.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

% ── Summary of findings ──
We have shown that multiscale VMR fingerprints provide a compact, interpretable
summary of cell type spatial organization in the mouse brain. Three properties
make this framework valuable.

% ── Robustness ──
\paragraph{Robustness.}
Fingerprints are stable to grid placement (CV~$< 5\%$ across 10 jitter trials)
and interior cropping. The extreme effect-size tail is dominated by cell types
that are rare visitors from adjacent brain regions, clustering tightly at their
primary anatomical location. When these are separated from region-native types,
the native effect sizes span a biologically interpretable range
(peak ratios 0.95--5.8$\times$ null for most types, where values below 1
indicate slight sub-Poisson regularity, with the habenular MH-LH Glut
reaching 36.4$\times$ due to tight nuclear confinement).

% ── Biology recovery ──
\paragraph{Known biology recovery.}
Subclass-level fingerprints in isocortex resolve the expected laminar hierarchy:
L2/3, L4/5, L5, L6, and L6b excitatory subtypes each show distinct clustering
scales. Glial and vascular types show characteristic small-scale
($\sim$\SI{40}{\micro\meter}) organization consistent with local microenvironment
patterning. Hippocampal fingerprints show stronger mesoscale compartmentalization
than isocortex, consistent with the highly structured dentate gyrus and CA
subfield architecture.

The sub-Poisson regularity observed for Astro-Epen (peak VMR ratio $0.95\times$
null) and Immune cells ($0.96\times$) is, to our knowledge, the first report of
below-random spatial regularity in spatial transcriptomics data. While
exclusion zone spacing is well-characterized in histological studies of retinal
mosaics \citep{Keeley2020}, it has not previously been detected at the
whole-brain tissue scale. The sub-Poisson signal is consistent with two
well-documented active spacing mechanisms. Protoplasmic astrocytes occupy non-overlapping
territorial domains with only 2--5\% volumetric overlap between neighbors
\citep{Bushong2002}, maintained through neuron-induced branch avoidance that
creates $\sim$40--60~\si{\micro\meter} exclusion zones
\citep{Hayashi2022, Halassa2007, Lanjakornsiripan2018}. Microglia exhibit
regular spacing generated by direct cell-cell contact-dependent repulsion
\citep{Endo2021, Davis2017}. Both mechanisms operate at the fine spatial scales
($\sim$40~\si{\micro\meter}) where we detect the sub-Poisson signal, and both
produce VMR~$< 1$ because territorial exclusion creates more regular spacing
than expected under random placement \citep{Keeley2020}. Notably, adult OPCs
also maintain territories through self-repulsive homeostatic spacing
\citep{Hughes2013}, consistent with the moderate OPC-Oligo residual (64.5\%
mediation) observed in our mediation analysis.

% ── Layered vs nuclear ──
\paragraph{Layered versus nuclear organization.}
The subclass-level co-occurrence comparison between isocortex and thalamus
reveals qualitatively distinct organizational motifs. Among 28 curated subclass pairs per region, cortical layer types are
overwhelmingly co-localized (96--100\% of pairs positive, depending on scale),
consistent with laminar stacking within shared cortical columns. In contrast,
thalamic nuclear types show weak, mixed correlations (46--64\% positive), with
more frequent scale-switching---consistent with a compartmental architecture
where distinct nuclei occupy non-overlapping territories. This suggests that the
framework can distinguish between fundamentally different modes of tissue
organization.

% ── Mediation ──
\paragraph{Stratum-level mediation quantifies architectural organizing principles.}
The mediation analysis transforms VMR fingerprinting from a detection tool into a
mechanistic decomposition. At the dendritic scale, a similar mediation framework
revealed that partner architecture mediates 25--53\% of excitatory but only
12--14\% of inhibitory regime coupling \citep{voruganti2026coupling}. Here, at the
tissue scale, we find an analogous asymmetry: neuronal types are almost entirely
explained by stratum membership (90--94\% in isocortex, 97--100\% in thalamus),
while non-neuronal types retain substantial within-stratum organization (vascular
mediation 39\% isocortex, $-44$\% thalamus). The parallel is structural: in both
systems, the ``simple'' cell classes (excitatory neurons on dendrites; neuronal
types in tissue) are well-explained by the coarsest organizing variable
(electrotonic regime; stratum membership), while the ``complex'' classes
(inhibitory neurons; non-neuronal tissue types) require finer-grained mediators
to explain their spatial patterns.

Notably, the 35\% isocortical residual is not uniformly distributed across cell
classes. Excitatory neuronal types retain only 6--10\% residual; inhibitory neuronal
types 10--22\%; glial types (Astro-Epen, OPC-Oligo, Immune) 21--35\%; and
vascular cells 61\%. The within-layer residual is concentrated in non-neuronal
classes, indicating that layer membership is a near-complete organizing variable
for neurons but not for glia or vasculature---consistent with non-neuronal cells
being organized by local niche features (e.g., neurovascular proximity, territorial
tiling) rather than laminar position alone.

The cross-architecture comparison (isocortex median 64.5\% vs.\ thalamus 91.0\%)
quantifies the fundamental difference between layered and nuclear tissue
organization. In layered cortex, cell types share space across columns and show
graded layer boundaries, leaving 35\% of the VMR signal unexplained by layer
alone. In nuclear thalamus, discrete non-overlapping territories almost fully
determine type identity \citep{Kapustina2024}. This difference was qualitatively
evident in the co-occurrence analysis (Section~\ref{sec:result3}); the mediation
framework now provides a single-number summary of the architectural distinction.

% ── Universality ──
\paragraph{Universality across biological scales.}
The same VMR/covariance-aware chi-squared framework has now been applied to three
distinct biological systems: protein nanoclusters at nm resolution (SMLM),
synaptic input clustering at \si{\micro\meter} resolution (dendritic trees), and
cell type organization at \si{\milli\meter} resolution (tissue sections). In each
case, the framework detects meaningful, scale-specific spatial structure with
appropriate null models (Fig.~\ref{fig:universal_comparison}). The mediation
extension demonstrates that the framework supports not only detection but also
mechanistic decomposition at the tissue scale, paralleling its use in decomposing
synaptic wiring mechanisms on dendrites. This convergence supports a universal
spatial fingerprinting and mediation toolkit for biology.

% ── Limitations ──
\paragraph{Limitations.}
Several limitations should be noted. First, in datasets of this size, nearly all
types are statistically significant, making $p$-values uninformative for
distinguishing biologically meaningful from trivial effects; we address this by
reporting effect sizes throughout. Second, Pearson correlation for co-occurrence
is simple but may miss more complex spatial relationships; future work could
incorporate mark correlation functions or cross-$K$ statistics.
We note that the standard alternative spatial statistic, Ripley's $K$/$L$ function,
is poorly suited to this context. A preliminary comparison of Besag's $L$
against a complete spatial randomness (CSR) null for 8 representative cell types
found that $L$ peaks at the maximum scale for all types, because the overall
tissue density gradient trivially violates CSR (data available in the
repository). The VMR framework avoids this by using a label-shuffle null that
preserves density structure and tests specifically for type-specific
organization, producing diverse, interpretable fingerprints where $K$/CSR
cannot discriminate between cell types. Third, our
primary analysis uses 2D projections of a 3D brain. The 3D thalamic comparison
(Section~\ref{sec:result4}) shows that most findings are robust to the projection
(mean 3D/2D ratio $= 1.32$), though z-laminated midbrain types show up to
$1.9\times$ stronger fingerprints in 3D. Extension to full 3D fingerprints would
be valuable for regions with strong z-axis lamination, particularly as
thick-tissue MERFISH methods improve axial resolution \citep{Fang2024}. Fourth, the mediation
analysis uses a chi-squared ratio to measure stratum-explained variance; this is
a statistical decomposition, not a causal demonstration that strata \emph{generate}
the fingerprints. Stratum assignment may correlate with other organizing variables
(local density gradients, vascular proximity) that are the true drivers.

% ── Future directions ──
\paragraph{Future directions.}
A systematic subclass-level analysis across all brain regions---beyond the
isocortex and thalamus focus here---could test whether the layered/nuclear
distinction extends to other organizational motifs (e.g., the nuclear but
layered cerebellar cortex). Integration with gene expression profiles could
reveal whether spatial fingerprint similarity predicts transcriptomic similarity.
Since continuous depth within layer explains only $\sim$4\% beyond discrete
layer labels (Section~\ref{sec:result4}), the 35\% isocortical residual is
primarily non-depth spatial structure. The mediation framework could be extended
to test tangential organizing variables: does local cell density, proximity to
vasculature, or cortical area identity mediate the within-layer residual?
Neurovascular niche features are a natural candidate, given that the residual
is concentrated in non-neuronal classes whose organization is known to depend
on vascular proximity: OPCs migrate along vasculature \citep{Tsai2016},
capillary-associated microglia show functional vessel coupling
\citep{Bisht2021}, and astrocyte endfeet provide near-complete coverage of
brain microvessels \citep{Mathiisen2010, Bushong2002}.
